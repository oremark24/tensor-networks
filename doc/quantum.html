5. Quantum Computing¶

Mathematics is a game played according to certain simple rules with meaningless marks on paper. David Hilbert

To understand Quantum Computing we need to know its realm and language. We study both first, Hilbert spaces and Dirac notation.

5.1. Hilbert spaces and Dirac notation¶

Hilbert spaces are attractive for physicists because they offer the possibility to incorporate infinitesimal changes into theories, enabling usage of derivatives, Taylor expansion et cetera. This founded by two properties of Hilbert spaces. They allow to measure distances, and ensure that all limit points (based on distances) belong to the space. How to measure distances? This is done through inner products, the mathematical model of angulometer and ruler for vectors. Note, that we consider only vector spaces over \(\C\) in this chapter, as this is used all the time in Quantum Computing.

Definition 5.1 (Inner product)

An inner product on vector space \(V\) over \(\C\) is a function

\[ \ip{\cdot}{\cdot}:V\times V\rightarrow\C,\,(u,v)\mapsto\ip{u}{v} \]

with following properties:

Additivity in second slot

\(\ip{u}{v+v'}=\ip{u}{v}+\ip{u}{v'}\) for all \(u,v,v'\in V\)

Homogeneity in second slot

\(\ip{u}{\lambda v}=\lambda\ip{u}{v}\) for all \(u,v\in V\) and all \(\lambda\in\C\)

Conjugate symmetry

\(\ip{u}{v}=\overline{\ip{v}{u}}\) for all \(u,v\in V\)

Positive definiteness

\(\ip{v}{v}\ge 0\) for all \(v\in V\) and \(\ip{v}{v}=0\) if and only if \(v=0\)

A vector space equipped with an inner product is called inner product space.

The question occurs, why did we define additivity and homogeneity only for the first variable? The answer is, for the second one, we can conclude similar characteristics from first slot properties and conjugate symmetry.

Observation 5.1 (First slot properties)

An inner product is additive and conjugate homogenious in second slot.

Additivity in first slot

\(\ip{u+u'}{v}=\ip{u}{v}+\ip{u'}{v}\) for all \(u,u',v\in V\)

Conjugate homogeneity in first slot

\(\ip{\lambda u}{v}=\overline{\lambda}\ip{u}{v}\) for all \(u,v\in V\) and all \(\lambda\in\C\)

Proof. For \(u,u',v\in V\) and \(\lambda\in\C\) we have

\[\begin{split} \ip{u+u'}{v} &= \overline{\ip{v}{u+u'}} \\ &= \overline{\ip{v}{u}+\ip{v}{u'}} \\ &= \overline{\ip{v}{u}}+\overline{\ip{v}{u'}} \\ &= \ip{u}{v}+\ip{u'}{v} \end{split}\]

as well as

\[\begin{split} \ip{\lambda u}{v} &= \overline{\ip{v}{\lambda u}} \\ &= \overline{\lambda\ip{v}{u}} \\ &= \overline{\lambda}\overline{\ip{v}{u}} \\ &= \overline{\lambda}\ip{u}{v}. \end{split}\]

The reason for having conjugate homogeneity in the first slot is that we want to obtain a real number, when applying the inner product of a vector to itself. This is necessary to define a feasible length measure based on the inner product.

Observation 5.2 (Self-product is real)

For each \(v\in V\) the inner product \(\ip{v}{v}\) is real.

Proof. Conjugate symmetry (Definition 5.1) yields

\[ \ip{v}{v} = \overline{\ip{v}{v}}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\ip{v}{v}\in\R\,. \]

Together Definition 5.1 and Observation 5.1 imply, that the inner product is kind of linear in the first variable and linear in the second variable. Hence, fixing the first variable yields a linear map in the second variable.

Observation 5.3 (Inner product as linear functional)

For a fixed \(u\in V\) the map

\[ \ip{u}{\cdot}:V\rightarrow\C,\,v\mapsto\ip{u}{v} \]

is a linear functional (linear map). For fixed bases \(\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}\subseteq V\) and \(\{1\}\subseteq\C\) the matrix representation of \(\ip{u}{\cdot}\) is

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \ip{u}{e_1}\;\ip{u}{e_2}\;\cdots\;\ip{u}{e_n} \end{bmatrix} \,. \]

Proof. The linearity follows directly from Definition 5.1. For \(v=v^ie_i\) we derive the matrix representation as usual:

\[\begin{split} \ip{u}{v} &= \ip{u}{v^ie_i} \\ &= v^i\ip{u}{e_i} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \ip{u}{e_1}\;\cdots\;\ip{u}{e_n} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v^1 \\ \vdots \\ v^n \end{bmatrix} \,. \end{split}\]

Based on the inner product we can define the length measure.

Definition 5.2 (Norm)

For \(v\in V\) we define the norm of v, denoted \(\norm{v}\), by

\[ \norm{v}\def\sqrt{\ip{v}{v}}\,. \]

Directly from this definition we obtain basic norm properties.

Observation 5.4 (Basic norm properties)

Well-definied

The norm is well-defined (not ambiguous) and \(\norm{v}\in\R\) for all \(v\in V\).

Absolute homogeneity

\(\norm{\lambda v}=\abs{\lambda}\,\norm{u}\) for all \(v\in V\) and all \(\lambda\in\C\)

Positive definiteness

\(\norm{v}\ge 0\) for all \(v\in V\) and \(\norm{v}=0\) if and only if \(v=0\)

Proof. The norm is well-defined and real because of Observation 5.2 and Definition 5.1 (positive definiteness). The last reference also includes positive definiteness of the norm.

Absolute homogeneity holds because of

(5.1)¶\[\begin{split}\norm{\lambda v}^2 &= \ip{\lambda v}{\lambda v} \\ &= \lambda \ip{\lambda v}{v} \\ &= \lambda\overline{\lambda}\ip{v}{v} \\ &= \abs{\lambda}^2\norm{v}^2\,.\end{split}\]

Equation (5.1) shows a very common technique when calculating with norms: working with squares and translating terms to inner products.

With help of the inner product we can also introduce the crucial definition of orthogonality.

Definition 5.3 (Orthogonality)

Two vectors \(u,v\in V\) with \(\ip{u}{v}=0\) are called orthogonal.

The origin of the word orthogonal is the Greek word orthogonios, which means right-angled. Already the ancient Greeks have dealt with “right angles”, that are also the basis of a classic theorem of that era.

Observation 5.5 (Theorem of Pythagoras)

Orthogonal vectors \(u,v\in V\) fulfill the equation

\[ \norm{u+v}^2=\norm{u}^2+\norm{v}^2 \,. \]

Proof. We have

\[\begin{split} \norm{u+v}^2 &= \ip{u+v}{u+v} \\ &= \ip{u}{u} + \underbrace{\ip{u}{v}}_{=0} + \underbrace{\ip{v}{u}}_{=0} + \ip{v}{v} \\ &= \norm{u}^2+\norm{v}^2 \,. \end{split}\]

The term norm is well defined even in more general terms (if not derived from inner product). Absolute homogeneity and positive definiteness are defining properties then. They are complemented and completed by a third defining property. That is the Triangle Inequality

(5.2)¶\[\norm{u+v}\le\norm{u}+\norm{v} \,,\]

that has to hold for all \(u,v\in V\). Similar to the other two properties, we will derive this automatically from the norm definition based on the inner product. But we have to work a bit to achieve this. We start with another famous inequality.

Observation 5.6 (Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality)

For \(u,v\in V\) we have

\[ \abs{\ip{u}{v}}\le\norm{u}\norm{v}\,. \]

The inequality becomes equal if and only if \(v=a\cdot u\) or \(u=a\cdot v\) for some \(a\in\C\).

Proof. Obviously, the assertion is fulfilled for \(v=0\). Now, assume \(v\neq 0\). We can write \(u\) as

\[ u= \underbrace{u-\frac{\ip{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}v}_{\fed w} + \underbrace{\frac{\ip{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}v}_{\fed w'} \,. \]

This is also called orthogonal decomposition, since \(w\) and \(w'\) are orthogonal:

\[\begin{split} \ip{w}{w'} &= \IP{u-\frac{\ip{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}v}{\frac{\ip{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}v} \\ &= \frac{\ip{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}\ip{u}{v}-\frac{\ip{u}{v}^2}{\norm{v}^4}\ip{v}{v} \\ &= \frac{\ip{u}{v}^2}{\norm{v}^2}-\frac{\ip{u}{v}^2}{\norm{v}^2} \\ &= 0 \,. \end{split}\]

Hence, we can apply the Theorem of Pythagoras Observation 5.5 to the orthogonal decomposition of \(u\).

\[\begin{split} \norm{u}^2 &= \norm{w}^2+\norm{w'}^2 \\ &= \norm{w}^2 + \Norm{\frac{\ip{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}v}^2 \\ &= \norm{w}^2 + \frac{\abs{\ip{u}{v}}^2}{\norm{v}^4}\norm{v}^2 \\ &= \underbrace{\norm{w}^2}_{\ge 0} + \frac{\abs{\ip{u}{v}}^2}{\norm{v}^2} \\ &\ge \frac{\abs{\ip{u}{v}}^2}{\norm{v}^2} \,. \end{split}\]

Multiplying this inequality by \(\norm{v}^2\) and taking square roots yields the Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality. Furthermore, we obtain that \(\norm{w}\) determines how strict the inequality is, having equality if and only if \(\norm{w}=0\). This is the case if and only if \(w=0\) or, by taking into account how \(w\) is defined, \(u=a\cdot v\) with \(a=\ip{u}{v}/\norm{v}^2\). This completes the proof.

This provides us with all the tools to proof the Triangle Inequality (5.2).

Observation 5.7 (Triangle Inequality)

For \(u,v\in V\) we have

\[ \norm{u+v}\le\norm{u}+\norm{v} \,. \]

The inequality becomes equal if and only if \(v=a\cdot u\) or \(u=a\cdot v\) for some \(a\ge 0\).

Proof. We have

\[\begin{split} \norm{u+v}^2 &= \ip{u+v}{u+v} \\ &= \ip{u}{u} + \ip{u}{v} + \ip{v}{u} + \ip{v}{v} \\ &= \ip{u}{u} + \ip{u}{v} + \overline{\ip{u}{v}} + \ip{v}{v} \\ &= \norm{u}^2 + \norm{v}^2 + 2\re{\ip{u}{v}} \\ &\underbrace{\le}_{\abs{x+iy}=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\ge\abs{x}} \norm{u}^2 + \norm{v}^2 + 2\abs{\ip{u}{v}} \\ &\underbrace{\le}_{\text{Cauchy-Schwarz}} \norm{u}^2 + \norm{v}^2 + 2\norm{u}\norm{v} \\ &= (\norm{u}+\norm{v})^2 \,. \end{split}\]

Taking square roots yields the Triangle Inequality. It is equal if and only if both inequalities in the equation chain are strict, that is if and only if \(\re{\ip{u}{v}}=\norm{u}\norm{v}\). If we have for example \(u=a\cdot v\) for \(a\ge 0\), then

\[ \re{\ip{u}{v}}=\re{\ip{av}{v}} =a\re{\underbrace{\ip{v}{v}}_{=\norm{v}^2\in\R}}=a\norm{v}^2 =\norm{av}\norm{v}=\norm{u}\norm{v}\,. \]

Conversely, \(\re{\ip{u}{v}}=\norm{u}\norm{v}\) implies the Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality being fulfilled with equality. Hence, we have for example \(u=a\cdot v\) with an \(a\in\C\). We put this into the equation:

\[ \re{\ip{av}{v}}=\norm{av}\norm{v}\,. \]

We transform the left-hand side

\[ \re{\ip{av}{v}}=\re{a\ip{v}{v}}=\re{a\underbrace{\norm{v}^2}_{\in\R}}=\re{a}\norm{v}^2 \]

and the right-hand side

\[ \norm{av}\norm{v}=\abs{a}\norm{v}^2 \]

to obtain

\[ \re{a}=\abs{a} \,. \]

This is only possible for \(a\in\R\) and \(a\ge 0\). The proof is complete.

Definition 5.4 (Hilbert space)

A Hilbert space \(H\) is is a complete inner product space.
